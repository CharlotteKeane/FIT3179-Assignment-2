Physical Activity in Australia

Author: Charlotte Keane

This data comes from the Australian Bureau of Statistics (ABS) and the Australian Institute of Health and Welfare (AIHW)

1. Diabetes Prevalence (2000–2021)

About This Data

Data from NIH and AIHW shows diabetes prevalence from 2000–2021, split by sex.

Over the past two decades, the prevalence of diabetes in Australia has steadily increased, affecting millions of people across all age groups. This rise highlights the importance of monitoring risk factors and promoting preventive measures at both individual and community levels.

Common actions to reduce Type 2 Diabetes risk include:

  • Maintaining a healthy diet rich in whole grains, fruits, and vegetables
  • Engaging in regular physical activity
  • Maintaining a healthy weight
  • Regular health checkups to monitor blood sugar
  • Reducing sedentary behaviour

We will focus on physical activity levels and rates in Australia.

2. Physical Activity Guidelines by Sex

Key Insights

This line chart shows trends in physical activity guideline adherence by sex from 2007–08 to 2022. Observations include:

  • Males have met their exercise goal more than females
  • Data was not measured between 2013 to 2017
  • After 2015 there was a significant increase in women meeting their exercise goal, coinciding with the decrease in diabetes seen in the initial graph.

While we have seen significant improvements in meeting exercise goals, only about 50% of people currently achieve them. Healthcare professionals can use this data to identify groups at risk and design targeted interventions, education programs, and support strategies to help more people meet physical activity guidelines.

3. Physical Guidelines Adherence by Age

Insights

The bar chart shows that many age groups do not regularly meet the recommended 30-minute daily exercise goal. The two worst-performing groups are 15–17 and 65+. Efforts should focus on encouraging older adults to engage in regular physical activity, as this could help reduce the prevalence of diabetes, which was reported at 16.9% in 2025.

4. Activity Levels: Cities vs Remote Areas

Insights

The chart shows differences among males living in major cities compared with those in outer regional and remote areas in meeting exercise guidelines, both including and excluding exercise at work. Female levels remained relatively consistent across locations, indicating that differences observed in males are not necessarily due to access to facilities.

6. Meeting Guidelines: At Work vs. Outside Work

Insights

The chart illustrates differences in physical activity levels between males and females across socioeconomic areas, showing whether individuals meet recommended exercise guidelines. For example, in the Northern Territory, exercise declines notably among people over 65, which aligns with the graph showing males’ activity excluding work decreases in lower socioeconomic areas. Low socioeconomic areas display a large gap between exercise at work and outside work, suggesting much activity comes from occupational tasks. In contrast, higher socioeconomic areas show smaller differences, indicating more consistent engagement in leisure-time exercise. These patterns highlight the need to encourage non-work physical activity, especially among older adults and in lower socioeconomic regions.

7. Types of Activity

Insights

This chart shows the breakdown of different types of physical activities undertaken by Australians. Observations include:

  • Walking and moderate-intensity exercise are the most common activities. Most likely due to how inexpensive it is.
  • Participation in vigorous-intensity activities is lower across most age groups.
  • These trends can inform targeted public health campaigns to increase engagement in moderate and vigorous activities where appropriate.
